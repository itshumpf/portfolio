sections:
  - title: "The coverage problem"
    body:
      - "Thirty-three sites, each staffed twice a day, is 66 discrete posts that have to be filled before the first bell and again at dismissal. A post is either covered or it isn't; there is no partial credit and no way to defer it to tomorrow. The roster of 50 guards had to absorb illness, weather, car trouble and no-shows without ever producing an empty corner."
      - "The schedule itself was the easy part. The hard part was the difference between the schedule and the morning."
  - title: "Hiring against churn"
    body:
      - "Crossing-guard work has notoriously severe turnover as a job category — comparable public programs consistently cite low pay, no benefits and a split shift that eats the middle of the day as the reasons. I made roughly 25 hires over two years against a 50-post roster, so about a quarter of the workforce turned over annually, which is well under what the category normally runs."
      - "Holding it there meant treating recruiting as continuous rather than reactive: interviewing before there was a vacancy, training people properly instead of handing them a vest, and being specific with candidates about what the shift actually is so the ones who took it stayed."
  - title: "Five constituencies"
    body:
      - "The job sat at the intersection of five groups with genuinely different incentives: the county sheriff's office, school principals, parents, the district, and the guards themselves. The sheriff cared about traffic enforcement and intersection design. Principals cared about their own bell schedule. Parents cared about one specific corner. The district cared about the contract. The guards cared about hours, weather and being treated like professionals."
      - "None of those were wrong, and they routinely pointed in different directions on the same intersection. Most of the work was translating between them early enough that a disagreement stayed a phone call instead of becoming a complaint."
  - title: "Same-day coverage"
    body:
      - "Coverage gaps were solved through relationships, not scheduling software. What actually filled a 7:15 post was a deep bench of people who would take a 6:45 call — in the rain, in January, on twenty-five minutes' notice — because the relationship was worth something to them."
      - "That bench was built deliberately over two years and it is the part that doesn't show up on an org chart. The zero-injury record and the unbroken coverage are downstream of it."

Blue Valley USD 229's southern half, two school years. The operational unit is one adult standing
at one intersection at one specific minute, repeated 66 times a day, every day school is in
session.

Everything below is about making that number hold.
